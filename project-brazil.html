Project Overview

As part of my AI4EO module during my MSc, I undertook a 3-week research project investigating whether satellite data could be used alongside ground-based observations to predict monthly groundwater storage changes in the Amazon basin. With a personal connection to Brazil through my father and a research interest in water sustainability, this proof-of-concept project explored the potential for satellite-based groundwater monitoring in remote, data-scarce regions.

I developed a machine learning framework to predict month-on-month groundwater level changes using GRACE-derived regional water storage, GLDAS land surface model components, and Sentinel-1 radar backscatter. Despite limited spatial coverage (only 4 wells) and temporal extent (2019 only due to time constraints), the approach achieved strong predictive performance (R² = 0.82), demonstrating the viability of satellite-based groundwater monitoring in tropical regions where traditional well networks are sparse.

Core Challenge: Data Scarcity and Time Constraints

Due to the remoteness of the study region, only four wells provided monthly groundwater change data for 2019. This sparse spatial coverage made interpolation across the region unreliable, requiring a point-based approach that averaged measurements. While this represented a significant spatial simplification given the Amazon's heterogeneity, it was deemed suitable for establishing proof-of-concept relationships between satellite observations and ground-based measurements in this densely forested environment given my lack of available time.

Methodology

Well Data Analysis

The well data represents monthly groundwater level changes, directly measured by the RIMAS monitoring network within Brazilian aquifers. Figure 2 illustrates the average month-on-month change in groundwater levels across the 4 wells that fall within my region of interest. It clearly depicts the hydrological cycle, with groundwater levels rosing during the wet season with a 1-2 month lag (peaking in June), then declining throughout the dry season (reaching minimum in December).

Groundwater Isolation from GRACE/GLDAS

GRACE provides monthly Total Water Storage (TWS) anomalies, representing all water stored on and beneath the land surface (groundwater, soil moisture, surface water, snow, canopy water). To isolate groundwater storage, I subtracted GLDAS-modeled surface components (soil moisture at multiple depths, snow water equivalent, canopy water) from GRACE TWS anomalies:

GWSA = TWS - (Soil Moisture + Snow Water + Canopy Water)

Month-on-month changes in these groundwater storage anomalies were then used as a predictive feature as seen in Figure 3.

Sentinel-1 Surface Conditions

Sentinel-1 backscatter (VV and VH polarisations) was averaged across the entire region to create point-based values consistent with the well data. These regional averages captured overall surface conditions related to vegetation and soil moisture dynamics, though the spatial simplification masked the Amazon's enormous heterogeneity. VH polarisation proved particularly valuable for capturing vegetation and surface water signals complementary to GRACE gravity measurements.

Model Performance & Key Findings

Three models were evaluated using Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation: Linear Regression, Ridge Regression, and Bayesian Ridge Regression. The Linear Regression model achieved the best performance with cross-validation R² of 0.82 and MAE of 17.5 ± 13 cm, successfully capturing seasonal dynamics from +80 cm during wet season peaks to -70 cm during dry season troughs.

Feature importance analysis revealed:

  • GRACE storage change dominated model performance, confirming the strong physical relationship between satellite gravity measurements and ground-based observations
  • Sentinel-1 VH backscatter emerged as the second most important feature, indicating that radar-derived vegetation/surface water signals provide valuable complementary information despite regional averaging
  • Seasonal patterns (sine/cosine month transformations) showed strong correlations but contributed moderate additional predictive value beyond GRACE

Conclusions & Future Potential

While constrained by limited spatial coverage and only a single year of observations, the results demonstrate the potential for satellite-based groundwater monitoring in data-scarce tropical regions. The dominance of GRACE in feature importance validates the physical basis of the approach, while the complementary value of Sentinel-1 suggests multi-sensor fusion can improve predictions beyond gravity measurements alone.

Future work could expand this proof-of-concept by: (1) incorporating expanded well networks and multi-year time series to improve model robustness, (2) utilising original 10m Sentinel-1 resolution with spatial interpolation of groundwater data to better capture the Amazon's spatial heterogeneity, and (3) exploring the approach in other data-scarce regions where satellite observations could supplement sparse ground networks. This research demonstrates that even with minimal ground truth data, satellite observations can provide valuable insights into groundwater dynamics in remote regions.
